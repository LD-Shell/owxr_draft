About Us

Overview of the Center

The Welch Center of Crystallization Research at the University of Houston is a world-class facility dedicated to the study of crystal formation mechanisms. Founded in 2015, the center brings together experts from Chemical Engineering, Chemistry, and Physics to solve complex problems related to nucleation, growth, and polymorphism.

Our research has direct applications in pharmaceutical manufacturing, preventing pathological crystallization (kidney stones, malaria), and optimizing petrochemical processes.

Our Research Themes

Our work is organized into three primary aims, tackling the lifecycle of a crystal from its birth to its final form.

01

Aim 1: Nucleation Mechanisms

We investigate the non-classical pathways of nucleation, using liquid-cell TEM and time-resolved spectroscopy to observe how molecules aggregate before a stable crystal lattice forms.

Liquid-Phase TEM Molecular Dynamics
02

Aim 2: Crystal Growth Modifiers

Understanding how impurities, proteins, and tailor-made additives bind to specific crystal faces to inhibit or promote growth. This is critical for controlling pathological crystallization like kidney stones.

AFM Peptide Design Microfluidics
03

Aim 3: Polymorphism & Stability

Many compounds can crystallize into different lattice structures (polymorphs). We study the thermodynamics and kinetics that determine which polymorph appears, crucial for pharmaceutical efficacy.

Raman Spectroscopy High-Throughput Screening

Center Receives $2M NIH Grant

September 15, 2024 | Grants & Funding

The Welch Center of Crystallization Research has been awarded a prestigious R01 grant from the National Institutes of Health (NIH) to investigate novel pathways of hematin crystallization in malaria parasites.


The Challenge

Malaria parasites survive by digesting hemoglobin, releasing toxic heme. They detoxify this heme by crystallizing it into hemozoin. Understanding the exact molecular mechanism of this crystallization is key to developing new antimalarial drugs that can inhibit this process.


Our Approach

Using the center's advanced liquid-cell transmission electron microscopy (TEM), our team will observe this crystallization process in real-time at the nanoscale. "This grant allows us to see what was previously invisible," says Dr. Peter Rimer, Principal Investigator.


The project is a collaboration between the College of Engineering and the College of Natural Sciences.
